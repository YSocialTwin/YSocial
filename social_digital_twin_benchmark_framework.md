# A Benchmark Framework for Social Digital Twin Simulation

## Purpose

This document outlines a paper whose contribution is **not only to validate YSocial**, but to define a **general benchmark framework for Social Digital Twin (SDT) simulation**.

In this framing:

- **Social Digital Twin** means a computational environment that simulates an online social platform by jointly modeling users, content, interactions, visibility, and network evolution.
- **YSocial** is the **case study** used to instantiate and demonstrate the benchmark.
- The benchmark is intended to assess **how reliable generated social simulations are relative to real-world platforms**.

The goal is to provide a reusable methodology that other SDT platforms can adopt.

---

## 1. Paper Objective

The paper should answer this question:

> How can we benchmark whether a Social Digital Twin generates social simulations that are reliable, stable, and plausibly comparable to real online platforms?

The contribution is a benchmark framework that specifies:

- **what** to benchmark
- **why** each benchmark dimension matters
- **how** to measure it
- **to what extent** agreement with real systems should be expected
- **what counts as a positive result** for each target variable

The benchmark should support two complementary goals:

1. **intra-simulator reliability**
   Can the simulator produce stable and interpretable outputs under controlled conditions?
2. **extra-simulator realism**
   Do the outputs fall within the empirical regime of real online social systems?

---

## 2. Benchmark Philosophy

The framework should avoid a simplistic “does the simulator perfectly reproduce platform X?” question.

That standard is usually wrong for at least three reasons:

- real platforms are themselves noisy, drifting, and policy-dependent
- many latent mechanisms are unobservable in real systems
- simulation is more useful as a generator of **reliable regimes** than as an exact replay engine

The benchmark should therefore assess reliability along **four dimensions**:

### B1. Stability

Does the simulator reproduce the same outcome regime under repeated runs with fixed settings?

### B2. Sensitivity

Do changes in meaningful parameters produce systematic and interpretable changes in outputs?

### B3. Robustness

Are results preserved under implementation-level substitutions that should not change social behavior, such as storage backend or runtime mode?

### B4. Realism

Do simulated observables fall within plausible ranges, shapes, and relationships seen in real platforms?

Together, these dimensions define benchmark quality.

---

## 3. Why a General Benchmark Is Needed

Social simulation papers often evaluate only one of the following:

- software correctness
- visual plausibility
- a single substantive finding
- anecdotal alignment with known social-media phenomena

That is not enough. A general benchmark is needed because SDTs are increasingly used for:

- hypothesis testing
- policy simulation
- recommender-system analysis
- synthetic data generation
- causal and counterfactual reasoning

These uses require evidence that a simulator is:

- not arbitrarily unstable
- not driven mainly by implementation artifacts
- not producing structurally unrealistic worlds
- not sensitive in opaque or uninterpretable ways

The benchmark should therefore serve the same role that common benchmarks serve in:

- forecasting
- generative modeling
- causal inference
- network modeling

It should make SDT reliability measurable rather than asserted.

## 4. What to Benchmark

The framework should benchmark outputs in six target domains.

## 4.1 Topology

These variables capture the structure of the social network generated by the simulator.

Target variables:

- node activity participation rate
- edge count and density
- in-degree and out-degree distributions
- reciprocity
- clustering coefficient
- assortativity by user attributes or interests
- modularity / community structure
- core-periphery concentration
- component size distribution

Why benchmark this:

- online social environments are fundamentally relational
- many downstream processes depend on topology
- unrealistic networks invalidate exposure, diffusion, and interaction results

## 4.2 User Behavior

These variables capture what simulated users do.

Target variables:

- posting rate
- commenting rate
- sharing/reposting rate
- reaction rate
- follow/unfollow rate
- session timing and daily activity cycles
- user-level activity inequality
- churn and newcomer behavior

Why benchmark this:

- platform realism depends on behavior, not only graph shape
- behavioral volume affects all content and network outcomes
- many research uses of SDTs focus directly on user activity

## 4.3 Content Production

These variables capture what kind of content is generated.

Target variables:

- content volume over time
- topic distribution
- topic persistence
- lexical or semantic diversity
- sentiment distribution
- toxicity distribution
- emotion distribution
- novelty vs repetition

Why benchmark this:

- synthetic societies are partly defined by their discursive output
- unrealistic content distributions can contaminate all downstream conclusions

## 4.4 Visibility and Exposure

These variables capture what users are likely to see.

Target variables:

- visibility inequality across users/posts
- exposure concentration
- exposure diversity
- follower vs non-follower exposure share
- topic exposure skew
- persistence of highly visible content
- exposure overlap across communities

Why benchmark this:

- the platform is not just what users post, but what they are exposed to
- recommenders operate mainly through visibility
- real social platforms are strongly shaped by uneven attention allocation

## 4.5 Interaction and Conversation Structure

These variables capture the form of social engagement.

Target variables:

- thread depth
- thread breadth
- reply latency
- reciprocity in conversations
- repeated interaction frequency
- cross-community interaction rate
- cascade size and structural virality

Why benchmark this:

- conversations are a core unit of platform dynamics
- many real platforms differ more in thread structure than in raw post volume

## 4.6 Opinion and Collective State

These variables capture emergent population-level alignment or fragmentation.

Target variables:

- opinion mean and variance
- polarization indices
- within-community opinion homogeneity
- between-community opinion distance
- convergence or persistent disagreement
- narrative concentration on salient topics

Why benchmark this:

- many SDTs are used to study influence, echo chambers, and polarization
- these are high-level outputs that integrate topology, exposure, and content

---

## 5. Benchmark Layers: What Exactly Is Being Evaluated

The framework should distinguish between four benchmark layers.

### Layer 1. Technical validity

Checks whether the simulator correctly instantiates the declared scenario.

Examples:

- config integrity
- logging completeness
- database consistency
- runtime completion
- no missing critical outputs

### Layer 2. Statistical reliability

Checks whether repeated runs under fixed settings remain stable.

Examples:

- low within-condition variance
- stable rank ordering across conditions
- repeatable regime-level outcomes

### Layer 3. Mechanistic validity

Checks whether parameter changes produce expected directional effects.

Examples:

- stronger follower prioritization increases follower-based exposure
- higher activity intensity increases posting and interaction volume
- stronger homophily increases assortativity

### Layer 4. Empirical plausibility

Checks whether the generated output resembles real platform regimes.

Examples:

- heavy-tailed activity
- concentrated visibility
- temporally uneven participation
- clustered interaction structure

---

## 6. How to Benchmark

The paper should define a benchmark protocol with four stages.

### Stage A. Controlled repeatability benchmark

Run the same scenario multiple times with fixed settings.

Measure:

- variance
- confidence intervals
- within-condition distances
- cluster separability from other conditions

Purpose:

- quantify intrinsic simulator noise

### Stage B. Parameter sensitivity benchmark

Vary major parameters and measure directional and effect-size changes.

Parameters:

- content recommender
- follow recommender
- initial topology
- activity schedule
- user heterogeneity
- memory
- opinion-dynamics parameters
- topic injection or exogenous news

Purpose:

- verify that the simulator responds to mechanisms rather than randomness

### Stage C. Implementation robustness benchmark

Keep the social scenario fixed while swapping implementation details.

Examples:

- SQLite vs PostgreSQL
- Redis off vs on
- local vs distributed runtime
- one client vs multiple clients

Purpose:

- ensure scientific conclusions do not collapse under engineering substitutions

### Stage D. Real-platform alignment benchmark

Compare simulator outputs to empirical ranges or stylized facts from real platforms.

Examples:

- degree/activity heavy tails
- visibility concentration
- bursty temporal cycles
- conversational depth distributions
- assortative social ties

Purpose:

- assess whether the simulator occupies the same qualitative and quantitative regime as real online systems

---

## 7. To What Extent Should Agreement with Real Platforms Be Expected

The benchmark must define realistic expectations.

### Expected level E1. Qualitative agreement

The simulator reproduces known stylized facts:

- skewed attention
- heterogeneous activity
- clustered communities
- temporal rhythms
- topic concentration

This is the minimum expectation.

### Expected level E2. Distributional agreement

The simulator reproduces the rough shape and scale of empirical distributions:

- similar heavy-tail behavior
- similar concentration levels
- similar thread-depth regimes
- similar assortativity range

This is the preferred target for a strong benchmark result.

### Expected level E3. Conditional relationship agreement

The simulator reproduces relationships between variables found in real systems:

- active users are more visible
- denser ties increase repeated interaction
- follower-centric feeds increase local exposure concentration
- homophily increases assortativity and reduces cross-group exposure

This is the most valuable benchmark target.

### Not expected

The framework should explicitly say that it does **not** require:

- exact event-level replay of real data
- exact matching of every marginal distribution
- exact matching across all platforms at once
- perfect temporal alignment with one real system

The benchmark is about **reliable correspondence in regime and mechanism**, not exact duplication.

## 8. Positive Outcomes: Variable-by-Variable Targets

This section is the core of the benchmark paper. It defines what a positive result looks like for each target domain.

## 8.1 Topology targets

### Degree distribution

What to benchmark:

- tail heaviness
- inequality
- rank concentration

Positive outcome:

- simulated degree distributions are right-skewed and concentrated in the same rough regime as real social graphs
- repeated runs under fixed settings preserve the same degree-inequality band
- topology changes directionally under recommender/topology interventions

### Reciprocity

What to benchmark:

- fraction of mutual ties

Positive outcome:

- reciprocity remains in a plausible platform-specific band
- follower-centric or social tie-based designs increase reciprocity relative to random baselines

### Clustering and modularity

What to benchmark:

- local clustering
- community detectability

Positive outcome:

- simulations generate non-trivial community structure
- modularity is stable under repeated runs
- homophily and follow-recommender interventions change modularity in expected directions

### Assortativity

What to benchmark:

- similarity of connected users by leaning, interests, or type

Positive outcome:

- assortativity is above random null expectation when homophily is modeled
- it remains stable enough to distinguish scenario classes

## 8.2 User-behavior targets

### Posting/commenting/reaction rates

What to benchmark:

- mean rates
- distribution across users
- temporal variation

Positive outcome:

- aggregate rates fall into plausible ranges
- user-level activity is heterogeneous rather than uniform
- activity cycles track daily or configured temporal rhythms
- repeated runs preserve the same regime

### Activity inequality

What to benchmark:

- Gini or similar inequality metrics over action counts

Positive outcome:

- a minority of users accounts for a disproportionate share of actions, as in real platforms
- inequality changes predictably with activity configuration or recommender exposure concentration

### Churn and newcomer dynamics

What to benchmark:

- inactivity, churn fraction, newcomer participation

Positive outcome:

- churn/newcomer processes do not create implausible population collapse or explosive growth unless intentionally configured
- retention and replacement dynamics are stable under repeated runs

## 8.3 Content targets

### Topic distribution

What to benchmark:

- share of content by topic
- topic entropy
- topic persistence over time

Positive outcome:

- seeded topics are visible but not frozen
- topic dominance emerges without immediate monopolization
- exogenous topic shocks change topical output in interpretable ways

### Sentiment / toxicity / emotion

What to benchmark:

- marginal distributions
- topic-conditional differences
- temporal persistence

Positive outcome:

- content-affect variables occupy plausible ranges
- toxicity or negative affect is concentrated rather than uniformly distributed
- interventions that alter conflict or memory produce directional changes

### Content diversity

What to benchmark:

- lexical diversity
- semantic diversity
- repetition rate

Positive outcome:

- the simulator does not collapse into excessive duplication
- different platform or recommender settings create distinct diversity regimes

## 8.4 Visibility targets

### Visibility inequality

What to benchmark:

- concentration of impressions/feed appearances across authors or posts

Positive outcome:

- visibility is uneven, as on real platforms, but stable within scenario
- recommender changes induce clear and interpretable shifts
- concentration is not purely random noise

### Exposure diversity

What to benchmark:

- diversity of seen authors, topics, and communities

Positive outcome:

- random or exploratory recommenders increase diversity
- follower-based recommenders reduce diversity but increase local coherence
- these contrasts are repeatable across runs

### Cross-group exposure

What to benchmark:

- exposure share to out-group content

Positive outcome:

- cross-group exposure varies systematically with recommender and homophily settings
- values remain plausible relative to empirical studies of selective exposure

## 8.5 Interaction targets

### Thread structure

What to benchmark:

- depth, breadth, and persistence of conversations

Positive outcome:

- thread-depth distribution is skewed, with many short threads and a smaller number of long ones
- memory, controversy, or comment-prioritizing feeds increase persistence in a controlled way

### Repeated interaction

What to benchmark:

- frequency of repeated pairwise engagements

Positive outcome:

- repeated interactions occur above random expectation
- follower-centric and memory-enabled conditions increase them

### Cascade dynamics

What to benchmark:

- repost/share cascade sizes
- structural virality

Positive outcome:

- most cascades are small, few are large
- popularity-biased feeds increase cascade concentration

## 8.6 Opinion targets

### Opinion dispersion

What to benchmark:

- spread and clustering of opinions

Positive outcome:

- opinion-dynamics settings induce measurable and stable differences
- bounded-confidence scenarios show clustering/consensus depending on epsilon
- no-opinion baselines do not spuriously produce the same structure

### Polarization

What to benchmark:

- bimodality
- ideological separation
- community-opinion alignment

Positive outcome:

- polarization metrics move in expected directions under selective exposure and homophily
- repeated runs preserve the same regime classification even if exact trajectories differ

## 9. Core Metrics and Evaluation Rules

The framework should define benchmark metrics and the logic for judging them.

### Stability metrics

- coefficient of variation
- bootstrap confidence intervals
- Wasserstein distance within condition
- intraclass correlation

Evaluation rule:

- a positive outcome means within-condition variation is substantially smaller than between-condition variation for core macro metrics

### Sensitivity metrics

- effect sizes
- variance decomposition
- regression coefficients
- Sobol indices or surrogate-model importance

Evaluation rule:

- a positive outcome means major parameters explain a meaningful share of variance in target outputs

### Robustness metrics

- equivalence testing
- distance between matched runs under infrastructure swaps
- rank-order preservation across scenario means

Evaluation rule:

- a positive outcome means implementation effects are smaller than main scientific effects

### Realism metrics

- KS/Wasserstein comparisons to empirical distributions
- stylized-fact checklists
- conditional-correlation comparisons
- ratio-of-inequality comparisons

Evaluation rule:

- a positive outcome means simulated outputs fall inside empirical or literature-supported plausibility bands for key observables

---

## 10. Benchmark Dataset Requirements

To compare SDTs with real systems, the framework should specify what real data are needed.

Minimum real-platform references:

- degree or follower distribution
- posting/activity distribution
- reply/thread statistics
- temporal activity cycles
- content/topic concentration
- visibility or proxy exposure concentration where available

These can come from:

- public platform datasets
- published empirical studies
- platform transparency reports
- benchmark collections from network science or CSS

The paper should allow **partial benchmarking** when some real variables are unavailable. Not every platform reveals exposure logs, for example.

---

## 11. YSocial as the Case Study

YSocial is a good case study because it exposes most benchmark-relevant surfaces:

- configurable content and follow recommenders
- configurable activity schedules and action probabilities
- explicit initial topologies
- opinion dynamics
- memory
- microblogging/forum variants
- distributed HPC execution
- structured logs and experiment artifacts

In the paper, YSocial should be used to show that the benchmark is executable in practice.

The case-study section should answer:

- can the benchmark be operationalized end-to-end?
- which dimensions does YSocial already satisfy strongly?
- where does YSocial remain weak or only partially validated?

That keeps the contribution general while grounding it in a real system.

## 13. Main Contribution Claim

The paper’s main claim should be:

> We introduce a benchmark framework for assessing Social Digital Twin simulations along four dimensions, stability, sensitivity, robustness, and realism, and demonstrate its use on YSocial as a case study. The framework identifies whether a simulator generates reliable social regimes, whether its mechanisms are interpretable, and whether its outputs remain within empirically plausible ranges relative to real online platforms.

That claim is broad enough to be general, but concrete enough to be testable.

---

## 14. Bottom Line

This benchmark paper should position itself as the **evaluation standard** for Social Digital Twins.

Its key message is:

- a good SDT is not one that merely “looks realistic”
- a good SDT is one that is:
  stable under repetition,
  sensitive in interpretable ways,
  robust to engineering substitutions,
  and empirically plausible relative to real platforms

YSocial then becomes the first substantial case study showing how such a benchmark can be instantiated in practice.
